\section{Situation-aware modeling}

\input{figures/data/h71e8/params.tex}

Although the previous simulations were important to compare the two techniques, showing that their performance is almost identical in both tested scenarios, appropriate results are still necessary in practical situations. In these, the following considerations are taken:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The OBNs positioned at the ocean floor act as sources, and a vehicle (either a ROV or a streamer) that traverses near the surface acts as a receiver;
	\item Vertically sensing the environment with ROVs isn't financially, and should be avoided;
	\item The speed of sound behaves linearly below the SOFAR channel, being feasible to model it by its depth with reasonable accuracy.
\end{itemize}

\input{figures/5.layered_model.tex}

In practice, both the vehicle and the OBNs act as sources and receivers, with the measurements being done over time for different vehicle positions, assessing the travel-time between the vehicle communicating with the OBN, this processing the commands, and responding. For simplicity, we treat different measurements as occurring simultaneously (assuming the environment's behavior is sufficiently stationary in time), and that the wave travel is only one-way. Also from the considerations, we assume that the receivers can be placed freely up to a $500\si{\meter}$ depth. This leads to a model as in \cref{fig:sec5:layered_model}, where there are 3 layers: A ($0-500\si{\meter}$), B ($500-1000\si{\meter}$), and C ($1000\si{\meter}-\text{floor}$). Notably, layer A is the one where the vehicles traverse, taking measurements, and layer C is the one that can be modeled \textit{a priori}, with its model being (possibly) refined in the optimization.

Given that the previous results show that both the literature and the proposed approaches are almost identical, the literature one will be chosen for now, given its more simple mathematical formulation. This will be coupled with the consideration of feasibly externally modeling the sound speed below a certain depth threshold.

\subsection{Split profile}
A different valid approach is to separate the SSP $\bvs$ into two vectors, $\bvs{1}$ and $\bvs{2}$, such that $\bvs{1}$ comprises the cells in the A and B layers, while $\bvs{2}$ those in the C layer; and, given that the velocity in the C layer can be modeled \textit{a priori}, $\bvs{2}$ doesn't need to be estimated. Assuming that this separation can be done such that $\bvs = \tr{[\bvs[T]{1}~ \bvs[T]{2}]}$ (that is, the initial elements of $\bvs$ correspond to the top two layers, and the remaining ones to the bottom layer), we then have
\begin{equations}
	E(\bvs{1})
	& = \bvs[T]{1} \bvR[T]{1} \bvR{1} \bvs{1} + \bvs[T]{1} \bvR[T]{1}\pts{\bvR{2}\bvs{2} - \bvt{\obs}} + \tr{\pts{\bvR{2}\bvs{2} - \bvt{\obs}}} \bvR{1} \bvs{1} + \norm{\bvR{2}\bvs{2} - \bvt{\obs}}^2 \\
	& + \alpha^2\pts{\bvs[T]{1} \bvD[T]{1} \bvD{1} \bvs{1} + \bvs[T]{1} \bvD[T]{1} \bvD{2} \bvs{2} + \bvs[T]{2} \bvD[T]{2} \bvD{1} \bvs{1} + \bvs[T]{2} \bvD[T]{2} \bvD{2} \bvs{2}}
\end{equations}
where $\bvR{1}$ comprises the initial columns of $\bvR$ related to the A and B layers, and $\bvR{2}$ the columns related to the C layer; and similarly for $\bvD{1}$ and $\bvD{2}$. The solution $\opt{\bvs{1}}$ that optimizes this cost function is
\begin{equation}
	\opt{\bvs{1;s}} = \inv*{\bvR[T]{1} \bvR{1} + \alpha^2 \bvD[T]{1} \bvD{1}} \pts{\bvR[T]{1}\bvt{\obs} - \bvR[T]{1}\bvR{2}\bvs{2} - \alpha^2 \bvD[T]{1} \bvD{2} \bvs{2}}
\end{equation}

Comparing to the original solution in \cref{eq:sec2:solution_regularization-problem_simple}, the inverse term (in brackets) is considerably similar, now only taking into account the $\bvs{1}$ layer. Meanwhile, the other term (in parenthesis) has additional elements: namely, the $\bvR[T]{1}\bvR{2}\bvs{2}$, related to the travel-time in the C layer; and $\alpha^2 \bvD[T]{1} \bvD{2} \bvs{2}$, related to the smoothness between the estimated (A and B) and guessed (C) layers.

While in this approach the slowness (and velocity) only needs to the estimated for the top two layers, the Eikonal solver for finding the traversed distances in each cell still needs to be updated for all cells, as the solution depends on $\bvR{2}$ (the distances in the bottom layer).

\subsection{Trade-off constraint}
In this approach, we use a secondary constraint on the minimization problem. While the $\alpha^2 \norm{\bvD \bvs}^2$ term is kept, improving the solution's smoothness, a secondary term will be added, restricting the error in the C layer between the estimated and a \textit{a priori} model. Mathematically, this is given by
\begin{equation}
	E(\bvs) = \norm{\bvR \bvs - \bvt{\obs}}^2 + \alpha^2 \norm{\bvD \bvs}^2 + \beta^2 \norm{\calI\pts{\bvs - \bvs{\obs}}}^2
\end{equation}
in which $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are regularization parameters; $\calI$ denotes an $\sz{N}{N}$ diagonal matrix, with its $i$-th element being $1$ if its corresponding cell (element of $\bvs$) is in the C layer, and $0$ otherwise; and $\bvs{\obs}$ is a model for the expected sound speed, only being relevant in the C layer. From the literature approach, the solution to this problem is
\begin{equation}
	\opt{\bvs{t}} = \inv*{\bvR[T] \bvR + \alpha^2 \bvD[T] \bvD + \beta^2 \tr{\calI} \calI} \pts{\bvR[T] \bvt{\obs} + \beta^2 \tr{\calI}\calI \bvs{\obs}}
\end{equation}

Note that only the $\bvR{i}$ term depends on the previous $\bvs{i}$ iteration, with the others being either model constants ($\bvD$ and $\calI$), measurement constants ($\bvt{\obs}$ is obtained from the surveys, and $\bvs{\obs}$ from known environmental data), and adjustable parameters ($\alpha$ and $\beta$). A larger $\alpha$ will favor a less varying environment, trading accuracy for smoothness; and a larger $\beta$ will push for a slowness profile that is closer to the model $\bvs{\obs}$.

Another possibility is for $\calI$ to more smoothly transition between the modeled (C) and non-modeled (A and B) layers. For example, it can be $0$ for cells at a depth of up to $900\si{\meter}$, $1$ below $1100\si{\meter}$, and have a transition step between these two depths. For now, we will take the discontinuous approach, switching at the $1\si{\kilo\meter}$ depth.

It is notable that $\opt{\bvs{t}}(\sigma,0) = \opt{\bvs{b}}(\sigma)$, and $\opt{\bvs{t}}(\sigma,\infty) = \opt{\bvs{1;s}}(\sigma)$, with the $\bvs{t}$ serving as a trade-off between the standard single constraint, and the split profile minimization. For this trade-off to be completely valid, it is necessary for $\calI$ to take the discontinuous approach, switching at the same depth as the split profile's $\bvR{1}$/$\bvR{2}$ separation. Another advantage of this trade-off format is it is considerably easier to include other (assumed known) layers into the fixed ones. For example, if one assumes that the top-most layer's parameters can be acquired with enough precision, and thus it can be approximated through Munk's model, this layer can be included into the second constraint by setting the appropriate entries in $\calI$ to $1$.

\subsection{Normalized forms}

Since $\bvR$ and $\bvD$ (as well as $\calI$) can have wildly different orders of magnitude, it may be useful to utilize normalized forms of each matrix. We denote $\bvR = \rho \bvR[t]$, $\bvD = \delta \bvD[t]$, and $\calI = \iota \calI[t]$. With this, the basic (standard; denoted $b$), trade-off (denoted $t$), and split (denoted $s$) minimization problems take the following forms:
\begin{subgather}
	\nabla E_b(\bvs) = 2\pts{\rho^2 \bvR[tT] \bvR[t]{i} \bvs - \rho \bvR[tT] \bvt{\obs} + \alpha^2 \delta^2 \bvD[tT] \bvD[t] \bvs } \\
	\nabla E_s(\bvs{1}) = 2\pts{\bts{\rho^2 \bvR[tT]{1} \bvR[t]{1} + \alpha^2 \delta^2 \bvD[tT] \bvD[t]} \bvs{1} - \bts{ \rho \bvR[tT]{1} \bvt{\obs} - \rho^2 \bvR[tT]{1} \bvR[t]{2} \bvs{2} - \alpha^2 \delta^2 \bvD[tT]{1} \bvD[t]{2} \bvs{2} } } \\
	\nabla E_t(\bvs) = 2\pts{ \bts{ \rho^2 \bvR[tT] \bvR[t] + \alpha^2 \delta^2 \bvD[tT] \bvD[t] + \beta^2 \iota^2 \calI[tT] \calI[t] } \bvs - \bts{\rho \bvR[tT] \bvt{\obs} + \beta^2 \iota^2 \calI[tT] \calI[t] \bvs{\obs}} }
\end{subgather}

We denote $\sigma = \frac{\alpha\delta}{\rho}$ and $\kappa = \frac{\beta\iota}{\rho}$, and with this,
\begin{subgather}
	\nabla E_b(\bvs) = 2\rho^2 \pts{\bvR[tT] \bvR[t]{i} \bvs - \frac{1}{\rho} \bvR[tT] \bvt{\obs} + \sigma^2 \bvD[tT] \bvD[t] \bvs } \\
	\nabla E_s(\bvs{1}) = 2\rho^2 \pts{\bts{\bvR[tT]{1} \bvR[t]{1} + \sigma^2 \bvD[tT] \bvD[t]} \bvs{1} - \bts{ \frac{1}{\rho} \bvR[tT]{1} \bvt{\obs} - \bvR[tT]{1} \bvR[t]{2} \bvs{2} - \sigma^2 \bvD[tT]{1} \bvD[t]{2} \bvs{2} } } \\
	\nabla E_t(\bvs) = 2\rho^2 \pts{ \bts{ \bvR[tT] \bvR[t] + \sigma^2 \bvD[tT] \bvD[t] + \kappa^2 \calI[tT] \calI[t] } \bvs - \bts{\frac{1}{\rho} \bvR[tT] \bvt{\obs} + \kappa^2 \calI[tT] \calI[t] \bvs{\obs}} }
\end{subgather}

Finally, the solution to each of these normalized problems is
\begin{subgather}
	\opt{\bvs{b}}(\sigma) = \frac{1}{\rho} \inv*{\bvR[tT] \bvR[t] + \sigma^2 \bvD[tT] \bvD[t]} \bvR[tT] \bvt{\obs} \\
	\opt{\bvs{1;s}}(\sigma) = \inv*{\bvR[tT]{1} \bvR[t]{1} + \sigma^2 \bvD[tT]{1} \bvD[t]{1}} \pts{\frac{1}{\rho} \bvR[tT]{1} \bvt{\obs} - \bvR[tT]{1}\bvR[t]{2}\bvs{2} - \sigma^2 \bvD[tT]{1} \bvD[t]{2} \bvs{2}} \\
	\opt{\bvs{t}}(\sigma,\kappa) = \inv*{\bvR[tT] \bvR[t] + \sigma^2 \bvD[tT] \bvD[t] + \kappa^2 \calI[tT] \calI[t]} \pts{\frac{1}{\rho} \bvR[tT] \bvt{\obs} + \kappa^2 \calI[tT] \calI[t] \bvs{\obs}}
\end{subgather}

Notice that these solutions are almost identical to the original ones, with a change of variables and the presence of the $\nfrac{1}{\rho}$ factor in front of $\bvR[tT] \bvt{\obs}$. Thus, the trade-off property is maintained, even after this transformation. Properly normalizing the matrices $\bvR$, $\bvD$ and $\calI$ allows for an easier and more standardized choice of the regularization parameters (now $\sigma$ and $\kappa$).

\subsection{Results}


Along with the previously established new technical considerations --- regarding the environment's layering, the approaches now considered, and the different results --- we also have more practical alterations in the simulated environment. Among them, is the consideration that depth and offset are limited to the $2\si{\kilo\meter}$ range, such that the maximum distance between devices is of less than $3\si{\kilo\meter}$ (the maximum OBN communication range), and that each device operates as either source or receiver, not as both simultaneously. We also extended the number of cells, from a $\sz{10}{30}$ grid to a $\sz{43}{50}$ one.

We will also compare four different distributions of receivers:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Case 1, with a layer of receivers near the surface;
	\item Case 2, with multiple layers of receivers in the top layer (layer A);
	\item Case 3, with the case 2 receivers + column of receivers along layers B and C;
	\item Case 4, With a column of sources in layer A along with the case 2 receivers.
\end{itemize}

These cases can be seen in \cref{fig:sec5:devices_distributions_all_cases}, comparing the four considered devices distributions, with sources as $\x$ in red, and receivers as $\bigcirc$ in yellow. The velocity field is also slightly different, compared to the one used in \cref{sec:simulation_and_results}, with less variation in the deeper parts (layer C), and more variation in the shallower parts (layers A and B).

The estimation results after 5 iterations (FMM for Eikonal solving + proposed technique for slowness/velocity estimation) are presented in \cref{fig:sec5:estimated_ssfs_all-cases_all-methods}, where we present the (signed) estimation error for all techniques. We see that the trade-off and split methods have very similar performance, in all scenarios. Even with external modeling for the layer C (with the trade-off and split methods), the results still present reasonable errors in estimating the SSF. We also see that the one with best results was case 3, with receivers positioned vertically and not only horizontally.

\subsection{Bullet-point discussion}

\begin{itemize}
	\item Trade-off and split have very similar performance.
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Related to the choice of parameters?
	\end{itemize}
	\item Adding more receiver layers (case 2 vs case 1) doesn't significantly improve the results.
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Overfitting?
		\item Too much information?
	\end{itemize}
	\item Adding vertically positioned receivers (case 3 vs case 2) improves the results.
	\begin{itemize}
		\item More spatially diverse information?
	\end{itemize}
	\item Adding some sources near the surface (case 4 vs case 2) doesn't significantly improve results.
	\item Best results are in case 3, specifically with the basic technique.
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Overfitting of trade-off/split methods?
		\item Needs better parameter tuning?
	\end{itemize}
